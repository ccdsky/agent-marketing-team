## The Five Strategy Skills

### 1. `/positioning-angles` — B2B Differentiation Frameworks

**What it does:** Generates 5-8 differentiated positioning angles based on research, scored by defensibility and ICP resonance.

**Thought leader foundations:**

**April Dunford (Obviously Awesome):**
- Positioning is not messaging — it's the context that makes your value obvious
- Her 5-component framework: Competitive Alternatives → Unique Attributes → Value (for customers) → Target Customers → Market Category
- B2B-specific: position against the *real* competitive alternative (often spreadsheets or manual processes, not another vendor)
- Key insight: most B2B companies position against the wrong competitor because they never ask buyers what they'd do without the product

**Peep Laja (Wynter):**
- 4-layer messaging test: Clarity → Relevance → Value → Differentiation
- 78% of B2B buyers have a shortlist before talking to sales — positioning must work before human contact
- In mature markets, you can't win by being "objectively better" — find a different consideration set or create a new one
- Message-market fit is as important as product-market fit

**Chris Walker (Refine Labs / Passetto):**
- Demand creation vs. demand capture — most B2B positioning focuses on capture (people already searching) while ignoring creation (making people aware they have a problem)
- "Dark social" — the influence channels you can't track (Slack, DMs, podcasts, word-of-mouth) often drive B2B decisions more than trackable channels

**Play Bigger (Category Design):**
- Category creation as positioning strategy — don't compete in an existing category, define a new one
- The "category king" captures 76% of the economics — being second is not a strategy
- Only viable for genuinely novel products; most B2B companies should position within an existing category with a twist

**What to AVOID:**
- Positioning by feature comparison (arms race you'll lose to bigger competitors)
- Internal committee consensus positioning (produces generic, undifferentiated copy)
- Copying the category leader's positioning (you'll always be perceived as #2)
- Positioning to "everyone" — B2B positioning must target a specific buyer within a specific context
- Changing positioning quarterly — consistency builds mental availability (Laja)
- "We're like X but better" — this anchors you to X's positioning framework

**Framework structure for SKILL.md:**
1. Map the real competitive landscape (Dunford: what would buyers do without you?)
2. Extract unique attributes (what can you truthfully claim that competitors can't?)
3. Map attributes to customer value (not features — outcomes)
4. Score each angle: Defensibility (can competitors copy this?) × ICP Resonance (does the target care?) × Provability (can you demonstrate this?)
5. Test via Laja's 4-layer framework: Is it Clear? Relevant? Valuable? Differentiated?
6. Present 5-8 scored angles with rationale, recommended top 2-3

### 2. `/keyword-research` — B2B Audience-Intent Research

**What it does:** Discovers high-value keyword opportunities scored by business potential, not just search volume. Produces a prioritized keyword map organized by buyer journey stage and content type.

**Thought leader foundations:**

**Ahrefs / Tim Soulo — Business Potential Score:**
- The single most important framework: score every keyword 0-3 on "Can I naturally demonstrate my product solving this problem?"
- 3 = product is irreplaceable solution. 2 = very helpful. 1 = barely relevant. 0 = no fit.
- Only target keywords scoring 2-3. Ahrefs grew from 15K to 700K+ monthly visits on this approach alone.
- Traffic Potential (total organic traffic the #1 page gets from ALL keywords) over raw search volume

**Rand Fishkin / SparkToro — Audience Intelligence:**
- 58-69% of Google searches end without a click (zero-click reality)
- HubSpot lost 80% of traffic while revenue held — traffic ≠ business outcome
- B2B buyers discover solutions through peers, communities, podcasts, not just Google
- Keyword research must be paired with audience research: where does the ICP actually spend attention?

**Andy Crestodina / Orbit Media — Money Phrases:**
- Two distinct tracks: "money phrases" (commercial intent for product/service pages) and "content keywords" (informational intent for blog/educational content)
- Sales calls are keyword goldmines — the questions prospects ask on calls reveal exact search language
- Topic-first, keyword-second: decide what your audience needs, then check if there's a keyword opportunity
- Orbit Media grew to $8M+ with zero ad spend on this approach

**Ross Simmonds / Foundation — Distribution-First:**
- "Create once, distribute forever" — keyword research without a distribution plan is incomplete
- For each target keyword, ask not just "can we rank?" but "where else can we distribute content about this?"
- Reddit increasingly appears in B2B SERPs — underutilized distribution channel

**Mike Sonders — Inverse Keyword Research:**
- Traditional keyword research is broken for B2B: tools treat queries as text strings without business context
- Inverse approach: start with business goals → generate topic hypotheses → validate with keyword data (not the reverse)
- Reduces research time from 15-40 hours to under 30 minutes with AI-native approach

**Eli Schwartz — Product-Led SEO:**
- Build SEO strategy around user problems, not keyword strings
- Discover non-obvious keywords by understanding what users are trying to accomplish
- A page that genuinely solves a problem will rank because it satisfies intent

**What to AVOID:**
- Sorting keyword lists by search volume (vanity metric in B2B — a 50/mo keyword with $50K deal value beats 5,000/mo tire-kicker keywords)
- Ignoring zero-click reality (some attractive keywords are fully answered in Google's SERP features)
- Using B2C methodology for B2B (volume + difficulty analysis without business potential scoring)
- Creating content for "everyone" instead of specific ICP segments
- Keywords without distribution plan (SEO is not the only way content reaches B2B buyers)
- Skipping SERP analysis (if DA90+ enterprises dominate, a DA20 startup needs a different strategy)
- Letting keyword tools drive strategy instead of business context

**Framework structure for SKILL.md:**
1. Define business goals and ICP problems (inverse research start)
2. Mine customer language (sales calls, support tickets, reviews — Crestodina's insight)
3. Discover audience hangouts (where do they consume content beyond Google? — Fishkin)
4. Generate topic hypotheses from problems, not keyword tools (Sonders)
5. Validate with keyword data (volume, Traffic Potential, Keyword Difficulty)
6. Score each keyword for Business Potential (0-3 — Soulo)
7. Check SERP reality (who ranks, zero-click features, AI Overviews)
8. Split into two tracks: Money Phrases (product pages) vs. Content Keywords (blog) — Crestodina
9. Map to buyer journey stage and stakeholder role
10. Plan distribution channels per topic (Simmonds)
11. Estimate pipeline/revenue impact
12. Output: Prioritized keyword map with editorial calendar

### 3. `/market-research` — B2B Competitive Intelligence

**What it does:** Executes a structured market research workflow with clear AI-executable phases and explicit flags for what requires human validation. Produces a research brief that feeds positioning, content strategy, and campaign design.

**Thought leader foundations:**

**Sean Ellis (GrowthHackers) — The 40% Test:**
- PMF survey: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" — 40%+ "very disappointed" = product-market fit
- The follow-up questions are the gold: "What is the main benefit?" gives value prop language in the customer's own words. "What alternative would you use?" maps the real competitive landscape.
- Superhuman: 22% → 58% PMF in 9 months by segmenting responses and building a 50/50 roadmap (double down on power users / remove friction for "somewhat disappointed" segment)

**Bob Moesta / Clayton Christensen — Jobs-to-be-Done:**
- Four Forces of Progress: Push (frustration with current state) + Pull (attraction to new solution) must overcome Anxiety (fear of switching) + Habit (comfort with status quo)
- "Only people have Jobs-to-be-Done, not organizations" — even in enterprise B2B, find the specific person willing to spend political capital to champion the purchase
- Career risk is a real force in B2B buying — "What if it goes wrong and I look bad?"
- The four forces directly generate messaging pillars: Address Push, Amplify Pull, Reduce Anxiety, Break Habit

**Adele Revella (Buyer Persona Institute) — 5 Rings of Buying Insight:**
- Priority Initiative (what triggers the search), Success Factors (expected outcomes), Perceived Barriers (what eliminates you), Buyer's Journey (who influences), Decision Criteria (what makes them choose)
- 77% of marketers never reference their own buyer personas — because most personas are fabricated from demographics, not researched from actual buying decisions
- Interview non-customers and lost deals, not just happy customers
- Core question: "Take me back to the day you decided to purchase..."

**Rand Fishkin / SparkToro — Audience Intelligence:**
- Observe what your audience actually does online (follows, reads, watches, discusses) instead of asking what they think
- Behavioral data at scale vs. self-reported survey data
- Identifies channels, creators, and publications that actually reach your ICP

**Peep Laja / Wynter — Message Testing:**
- 4-layer test: Clarity → Relevance → Value → Differentiation
- Test with verified category buyers, not internal stakeholders
- Brand tracking every 6-12 months: unaided awareness, aided awareness, perception, consideration

**The critical cross-cutting insight (all five agree):**
The most dangerous market research mistake is asking people what they want instead of studying what they do. Ellis measures disappointment, not satisfaction. Moesta reconstructs purchase stories, not feature wishlists. Revella follows buyer narratives, not scripts. Fishkin observes behavior, not self-reports. Laja tests with real buyers, not internal committees.

**Implication for AI agents:** Observational desk research (what people DO) is often more reliable than self-reported data (what people SAY). This makes the AI agent's desk research capability genuinely valuable, not a poor substitute.

**What to AVOID:**
- The "made up persona" trap — filling templates with demographics instead of interviewing actual buyers (Revella)
- Surveying only happy customers — missing the complete market picture from lost deals and non-customers
- Confusing demographics with insights — job title and company size don't drive buying decisions
- Ignoring the status quo as primary competitor — in B2B, "do nothing" is usually the biggest competitor (Moesta)
- Testing messaging internally — committee consensus produces generic, undifferentiated copy (Laja)
- One-time research — markets shift, buyer language changes, competitors evolve
- Over-indexing on search data alone — missing the 58.5% of searches that end without clicks and influence from non-search channels

**Framework structure for SKILL.md (phased, AI-honest about limitations):**

Phase 1: LANDSCAPE (fully AI-executable)
- Market mapping and sizing from public data
- Competitor identification and profiling
- Positioning analysis across competitor websites
- Technology and trend scanning

Phase 2: DEEP INTELLIGENCE (AI-executable with tool access)
- Systematic competitor messaging analysis (Laja's 4-layer framework)
- Customer language extraction organized by JTBD forces (push/pull/anxiety/habit)
- Review mining (G2, Capterra, Reddit) for buyer language at scale
- Audience behavior mapping (SparkToro-style: where does the ICP consume content?)
- Content gap analysis and opportunity identification

Phase 3: RESEARCH BRIEF (AI synthesis)
- Compiled findings structured by the five research lenses
- Positioning hypotheses with supporting evidence
- Draft messaging options for testing
- Explicit "primary research needed" flags with specific questions

Phase 4: PRIMARY RESEARCH SUPPORT (human leads, AI assists)
- Generate JTBD interview guides based on desk research findings
- Design PMF survey instruments (Ellis framework)
- Draft message test variants for Wynter-style testing
- Analyze interview transcripts for themes

### 4. `/expert-review` — Multi-Agent Specialized Analysis

**What it does:** Spawns 3-5 specialized review agents to analyze a draft asset from different expert perspectives. Synthesizes feedback into actionable revision guidance.

**Why it matters for B2B:**
B2B marketing assets must satisfy multiple stakeholders (end user, manager, executive, procurement) and pass through committees. A single reviewer catches single-perspective issues. Multi-agent review catches cross-audience problems before they reach real buyers.

**Expert perspectives for B2B (configurable per asset type):**

For **landing pages:**
- Conversion copywriter (CTA strength, objection handling, urgency)
- Exposure Ninja's CLOSURE framework auditor (Credibility, Lure, Objection, Social proof, Usability, Result)
- ICP relevance reviewer (speaks to their pain? uses their language?)
- Voice & tone matcher (compare to voice-dna.md)
- B2B buyer psychology reviewer (addresses career risk? reduces switching anxiety? — Moesta's four forces)

For **lead magnets:**
- Hormozi Value Equation scorer (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay × Effort)
- "Bridge" reviewer (does solving the narrow problem reveal the next problem your core offer solves?)
- Completeness reviewer (complete solution to narrow problem, not teaser?)
- Exposure Ninja Three Pillars checker (Relevancy, Perceived Value, True to Your Word)
- B2B applicability reviewer (implementable by the ICP? relevant to their business context?)

For **email sequences:**
- Sequence arc reviewer (does the narrative build across emails?)
- Subject line specialist (open rate optimization)
- CTA reviewer (one clear action per email?)
- Unsubscribe risk reviewer (which emails might trigger opt-outs?)
- B2B buying cycle aligner (does timing match how B2B buyers actually evaluate?)

**What to AVOID:**
- Running expert review on Sprint 1 sketches (too early — wait for Sprint 2 full drafts)
- Treating expert feedback as prescriptive (it's input for Creative Specialist, not direct edits)
- More than 5 experts per asset (diminishing returns, conflicting advice)
- Generic expert prompts (each expert must have a specific lens and specific evaluation criteria)

**Framework structure for SKILL.md:**
1. Determine asset type → select appropriate expert panel
2. Spawn experts in parallel (Task tool, 3-5 agents)
3. Each expert evaluates against their specific framework/criteria
4. Synthesize: consensus strengths, consensus issues, conflicting opinions with recommendation
5. Output: prioritized revision list with specific changes, estimated impact per change

### 5. `/lead-magnet-strategy` — B2B Lead Magnet Concept Design

**What it does:** Designs high-converting lead magnet concepts based on the business's core offer, ICP pain points, and competitive landscape. Produces a scored concept brief before any content creation begins.

**Thought leader foundations:**

**Alex Hormozi ($100M Leads) — The Bridge Framework:**
- Master definition: "A complete solution to a narrow problem that, once solved, reveals another problem solved by your core offer."
- Every word matters: Complete (not a teaser). Narrow (not "how to grow your business"). Reveals (creates awareness of the next problem). Solved by your core offer (natural bridge).
- The lead magnet IS the sales process — it does the persuasion work by demonstrating competence

**Hormozi — The Value Equation:**
- Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
- Competitive advantage comes from the denominator — making things faster and easier
- A calculator that gives an answer in 30 seconds is structurally more valuable than a 20-page guide (even if the guide contains more information)
- "If your lead magnet is not good enough that people tell other people about it, you have failed"

**Hormozi — Three Lead Magnet Types:**
1. Problem Revealers (Diagnosis) — show a problem they didn't know they had. B2B gold: free audits, assessments, benchmarking tools, gap analyses
2. Samples & Trials — solve a recurring problem for a limited time. B2B gold: free pilot, limited feature access, one free campaign setup
3. One Step of a Multi-Step Process — give away one valuable step, sell the complete process. B2B gold: free strategy session, free architecture review

**Hormozi — "Give Away the Secrets, Sell the Implementation":**
- Information is commoditized. Your secrets are on YouTube.
- What people pay for: implementation, speed, accountability, convenience
- If the free thing is incredible, they think "if this is free, imagine what I get when I pay"
- Giving away secrets filters for your BEST customers — those who value implementation

**Exposure Ninja — CLOSURE + Train Journey:**
- Train Journey Principle: your offer should be the next logical station, not the final destination
- Bad: "Contact us." Good: "We'll show you exactly how much you could save on your IT costs."
- Five lead magnet archetypes ranked for B2B: Consultative First Step > Quiz/Calculator > Free Course > Checklist/Template > Freewall
- Three Pillars test: Relevancy (aligned with audience needs) + Perceived Value (worth sharing contact info) + True to Your Word (delivers on promise)
- Their own lead magnet (free video website review) generates 4,000+ leads/year — high-effort but high-conversion consultative first step

**Exposure Ninja — Homer Simpson Principle:**
- Design for the distracted, confused visitor
- Minimum friction: Name + email only. No account creation. Mobile-friendly. Instant delivery.
- Each additional form field reduces completion rates

**What to AVOID:**
- Packaging over content — spending weeks designing a PDF with no real value (Hormozi)
- Teaser-style content — giving an incomplete solution to a broad problem instead of a complete solution to a narrow one (Hormozi)
- Generic ebooks — modern audiences expect substantial, interactive, practical value (Exposure Ninja)
- No clear bridge to core offer — the lead magnet generates goodwill but not revenue (Hormozi)
- Un-tested naming — not testing headlines before building the full asset (Hormozi: "5x more people read your headline")
- Making it hard to consume — requiring account creation, multi-step download processes, desktop-only formats (Hormozi)
- Low-value lead magnets — weak magnets damage your brand across ALL channels (Hormozi)
- "Contact us" as the CTA — too much commitment too early. The Train Journey Principle says: offer the next station, not the final destination (Exposure Ninja)
- Irrelevant CTAs — a blog about Topic X with a lead magnet about Topic Y always underperforms. "Relevancy is the blood of a blog's anatomy" (Exposure Ninja)

**Framework structure for SKILL.md:**
1. Define the Bridge: narrow problem → problem revealed → core offer connection
2. Select lead magnet type: Problem Revealer, Sample/Trial, or One Step (match to business model)
3. Select format: Software/Tool, Information, Service, Physical (B2B bias toward Software and Service)
4. Score with Value Equation: Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay × Effort (minimum threshold before proceeding)
5. Apply Exposure Ninja's Three Pillars test: Relevancy? Perceived Value? True to Your Word?
6. Test the name: 3 headline options, evaluate before building
7. Design delivery: instant, frictionless, mobile-friendly (Homer Simpson Principle)
8. Map distribution: how does the lead magnet reach the ICP? Blog CTAs, LinkedIn, paid, email, partnerships
9. Design the nurture bridge: what happens after opt-in? (email sequence that continues the reveal)
10. Quality gate: "Would someone pay for this?" If no, redesign before building.
