# Ideal Customer Profile — Open Source Integrators (OSI)

> **For all agents:** OSI sells to a buying committee, not a single buyer. A deal typically involves 2–3 of the personas below. Research which persona is the primary champion before writing. Adjust the lead pain and lead outcome per persona — the voice stays the same across all three.

---

## The Buying Committee

OSI's typical deal involves a buying committee across Finance, Operations, and the CEO. The **CFO or VP Ops** usually initiates the conversation (the pain is immediate and operational). The **CEO** approves the investment. All three must be comfortable before a contract is signed.

| Persona | Initiator? | Approver? | Primary fear |
|---|---|---|---|
| CFO / VP Finance | Often | Yes | Budget overrun, hidden costs, audit risk |
| VP Operations / COO | Often | Influencer | Failed implementation, team resistance |
| CEO | Rarely | Yes | Strategic wrong bet, distraction from growth |

---

## Persona 1: CFO / VP Finance

### Who They Are
**Titles:** CFO, VP Finance, Controller, Finance Director, VP of Accounting
**Company size:** $10M–$500M revenue (mid-market)
**Industries:** Manufacturing, Distribution, Logistics, Construction, Engineering, Aerospace
**Reports to:** CEO / Board

### Their Reality Right Now
They run finance on a combination of systems that don't talk to each other. Month-end close is a two-to-three-week fire drill. Consolidated reporting across entities means exporting data from multiple systems and reconciling in Excel. They know the numbers are always a little wrong — they just don't know where.

The legacy system (SAGE, QuickBooks Enterprise, Dynamics NAV, or something even older) was "good enough" until the business grew past it. Now it's a liability. Support is expensive. Customizations from 2009 break every time there's an upgrade. Vendor lock-in means no leverage.

### Their Pain Points
- Month-end close takes 2–4 weeks, requires heroic manual effort
- No real-time consolidated view across entities / subsidiaries
- Financial data lives partially in the ERP, partially in spreadsheets
- Legacy system maintenance and licensing costs are rising with no end in sight
- Audit trail is unclear — recreating transactions is painful
- Budget vs. actuals require manual pulls and reconciliation
- They can't trust the numbers without verifying them personally

### Their Goals
- Real-time financial visibility — know the numbers without asking
- Month-end close in days, not weeks
- Consolidated multi-entity reporting without spreadsheet heroics
- Reduce total cost of ownership (licensing + maintenance + custom dev)
- A clean audit trail they can defend
- A system they can grow into, not out of

### The Language They Use (Verbatim)
> "We're flying blind between reporting periods."
> "Month-end close takes my whole team two and a half weeks."
> "We're running the books on QuickBooks and a lot of Excel."
> "Every time we try to upgrade, something breaks."
> "I can't get a consolidated P&L across our entities without manually pulling it together."
> "What's the total cost of ownership — including implementation?"
> "We went through an ERP implementation eight years ago. It was a disaster."
> "I need to know what's hidden in the pricing."

### Their Decision Criteria
1. **Total cost certainty** — licensing, implementation, training, ongoing support. No surprises.
2. **Implementation risk** — who else have you done this for? Can I talk to them?
3. **Timeline realism** — they've been promised "90 days to go-live" before. They're skeptical.
4. **Industry references** — they want to talk to a CFO in a similar company, not read a generic case study.
5. **Audit and compliance** — will this make an audit easier or harder?

### Their Objections
- "We went through this before and it cost twice what was quoted and took twice as long."
- "Our team is already stretched thin — who's going to manage this implementation?"
- "What's actually included in your price? What will you charge us for that seems like it should be included?"
- "Can this actually handle multi-entity / multi-currency? Or is that an add-on?"
- "Why Odoo? We've never heard of it."

### How to Earn Their Trust
- Named client references in similar industries. Specific numbers. Let them call.
- Transparent, itemized pricing. The CFO's job is to find the hidden costs — show them there aren't any.
- Honest about what's hard. If implementation is going to require X months of their team's time, say so upfront.
- Show the total cost of ownership math explicitly, including what they're spending now.

## Persona 2: VP Operations / COO

### Who They Are
**Titles:** VP Operations, COO, Director of Operations, VP Supply Chain, VP Manufacturing, Plant Manager (decision influence)
**Company size:** $10M–$500M revenue
**Industries:** Manufacturing, Logistics, Construction, Distribution, Aerospace, Engineering
**Reports to:** CEO

### Their Reality Right Now
Operations are held together with disconnected systems and manual handoffs. Inventory numbers in the system don't match what's on the shelf. Production planning is done in a spreadsheet that one person maintains. When a customer order comes in, it takes multiple emails across multiple departments to confirm whether it can actually be fulfilled.

Their team is smart and works hard. The problem isn't the people — it's the systems. But they've also seen ERP implementations destroy team morale and productivity. They know what a failed go-live looks like. They're not going to put their team through that without strong reason to believe it'll work.

### Their Pain Points
- Inventory accuracy is unreliable — physical counts don't match system records
- Production planning lives in spreadsheets; schedule changes require manual cascade updates
- "The integration layer between systems is email" — data moves by people, not automation
- Operations visibility is always delayed — they know what happened last week, not today
- New employee onboarding into disconnected systems takes weeks of training
- Every department runs a different system; nothing talks to anything else natively
- Customer orders require manual verification across 3+ systems before promising a delivery date

### Their Goals
- Real-time operational visibility — know what's happening on the floor without asking
- Inventory that's accurate — what's in the system matches what's on the shelf
- Fewer manual touches — transactions that post automatically, not by hand
- One system that operations, warehouse, and finance all share
- Production planning that updates dynamically when inputs change
- An implementation that doesn't destroy their team's productivity during go-live

### The Language They Use (Verbatim)
> "I find out too late."
> "Excel is our integration layer."
> "We're doing this manually and it's killing us."
> "I have no visibility until end of day — or end of week."
> "The handoff between sales and ops breaks down every time."
> "We have to re-enter everything into three different systems."
> "My team spends half their time reconciling data instead of doing their actual job."
> "We tried to implement [previous system] and it was a nightmare. I'm not doing that again."

### Their Decision Criteria
1. **Industry fit** — Does OSI understand their specific operational model (manufacturing vs. distribution vs. construction)? Do they have references in the same industry?
2. **Implementation reality** — What does the go-live actually look like? How disruptive is it? What does the team have to do?
3. **User adoption** — Will their team actually use it? How hard is Odoo to learn?
4. **Post-go-live support** — What happens when something breaks at 7am on a Monday?
5. **Timeline** — How long until they're fully operational?

### Their Objections
- "My team doesn't have capacity to manage an implementation on top of their day jobs."
- "We tried this before and the system we implemented is still not fully live two years later."
- "Change management will be brutal. My team is resistant."
- "How do you handle [specific operational complexity — e.g., lot tracking, multi-warehouse, project-based billing]?"
- "What happens if Odoo doesn't do exactly what we need? Can it be customized?"

### How to Earn Their Trust
- Speak their operational language. Know what lot tracking, MRP, BOM, 3PL, and landed cost mean without explanation.
- Industry references they can call — not just logos, actual conversations with operations leaders.
- Be specific about implementation methodology — what happens in week 1, week 4, week 8.
- Acknowledge change management is real. Show how you've handled it before.
- Prove Odoo's customizability without overpromising what's out of the box.

## Persona 3: CEO / President

### Who They Are
**Titles:** CEO, President, Founder/CEO, Managing Director, Owner
**Company size:** $10M–$500M revenue
**Industries:** Manufacturing, Logistics, Engineering, Construction, Distribution, Aerospace
**Relationship to ERP decision:** Strategic approver, not operational champion

### Their Reality Right Now
The business has grown past the systems that got it here. What worked at $20M in revenue is breaking at $60M. They're adding headcount to compensate for system limitations instead of scaling efficiently. They're spending time managing operational fires that should be managed by their team — because the data isn't clean and nobody trusts the systems.

The CEO doesn't initiate the ERP conversation — the CFO or VP Ops does. But the CEO has to approve the budget and the risk. Their question is: is this the right bet at the right time, and is OSI the right partner?

### Their Pain Points
- Can't get a clear picture of the business without calling five different people
- Growth is outpacing the systems — complexity is accelerating, but systems aren't keeping up
- Spends too much time on operational decisions that should be delegated
- Board / investors ask for data that takes weeks to pull together
- Competitors who've invested in systems are moving faster and winning bids they used to win
- Adding headcount to fix system problems instead of scaling the business

### Their Goals
- Visibility across the entire business — one number, one source of truth
- Systems that scale with growth without proportional headcount increases
- More time on strategy; less time managing fires that bad systems cause
- A partner relationship, not a vendor transaction
- Competitive position — not falling behind competitors who run leaner operations

### The Language They Use (Verbatim)
> "I'm spending too much time managing operations instead of growing the business."
> "We can't scale on the systems we have."
> "I need to see the business clearly without having to call five people."
> "We keep adding headcount to compensate for bad systems."
> "Is now the right time to do this?"
> "We've had bad implementations before. I need to know this one is different."
> "I don't want another vendor. I want a partner who's in this with us."

### Their Decision Criteria
1. **Partner credibility** — Is OSI a real partner, or a software reseller with a services team attached? Can they handle complexity without coming back to the CEO every week?
2. **Risk level** — What's the realistic downside scenario? How has OSI handled implementations that hit trouble?
3. **Strategic fit** — Is Odoo the right foundation for the next 10 years, not just the next 3?
4. **ROI framing** — Not just cost savings, but growth capacity. What does this make possible that isn't possible today?
5. **Team confidence** — Does the CFO trust the numbers? Does the VP Ops think this will actually work?

### Their Objections
- "We went through a bad implementation before. How do I know this is different?"
- "Is now the right time to do this, given everything else we're managing?"
- "What's my exposure if this goes sideways?"
- "Why Odoo and not SAP / NetSuite / Dynamics?"
- "How long until we see ROI? I need to justify this to the board."

### How to Earn Their Trust
- Don't lead with features. Lead with what becomes possible: "Companies that implement Odoo at your scale typically get to X."
- Give them a realistic downside scenario and explain how OSI mitigates it. Hiding risk destroys trust.
- Position as a long-term partner: open source means no vendor lock-in, OSI means ongoing relationship.
- Let them talk to other CEOs who made this bet. Not case studies — phone calls.
- Frame ROI in terms of growth capacity, not just cost reduction.

## Shared Triggers Across All Personas

These pain points resonate across all three buyer types. Use them in top-of-funnel content that hasn't yet been personalized to a specific persona.

- **"We're running the business on disconnected systems."** — Universal
- **"I find out too late."** — Universal
- **"We keep adding headcount to fix system problems."** — Universal
- **"Our last implementation was a disaster."** — Universal fear
- **"What's hidden in the pricing?"** — Universal skepticism
- **"We need to scale and our current systems can't do it."** — Universal growth pressure

## What OSI Does NOT Sell To

Understanding the anti-ICP protects content from being generic:

- **Enterprise (Fortune 500):** They need SAP, Oracle, or Dynamics. OSI isn't positioned for them.
- **Very early stage (<$5M revenue):** Too small for the complexity and cost of a full implementation.
- **Non-operational businesses:** Pure professional services firms with no inventory, no production, no complex operations aren't the sweet spot.
- **Single-function buyers:** Someone who only needs accounting software (not operations integration) isn't an ERP buyer.

## Channels and Where They Spend Time

| Persona | Primary channels | Content they trust |
|---|---|---|
| CFO / Finance | LinkedIn, industry CFO groups, peer referrals, Gartner / G2 reviews | Peer references, ROI analysis, case studies with numbers |
| VP Ops / COO | LinkedIn, industry operations forums, peer referrals, trade publications | Implementation methodology docs, technical deep-dives, operational case studies |
| CEO | LinkedIn (light), CEO peer groups (EO, YPO), direct referrals | Brief, outcome-focused content; they don't read long white papers |

**Key insight:** All three personas trust peer referrals far more than any content OSI produces. Content's job is to get them to the referral call, not to close the deal.
